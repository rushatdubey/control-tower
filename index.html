A distribution network fails one lane at a time, long before finance sees the bill.

Across 180,000 orders and six distribution centres, the network-wide on-time rate looks healthy. It hides the fact that a single DC is quietly destroying the number, and by month-end the SLA penalties are already spent. This is the case for watching the network daily, not monthly.

01 / THE BLIND SPOT

The average is healthy. The network is not.

The network delivers on-time-in-full 82% of the time, a respectable headline. But OTIF is an average of six very different operations. One DC runs at 91%; another sits at 68%. A single aggregate number lets a failing site hide inside a passing network until the cost lands on the P&L.

02 / THE REAL CULPRIT

It isn't slowness. It's unpredictability.

DC-3's average transit time is barely worse than the network. What breaks it is variance: its delivery window swings twice as wide as any other site. A customer can plan around a slow-but-steady lane. They cannot plan around one that is sometimes fast and sometimes very late. Variance, not the mean, is what turns into a missed SLA.

Why variance, not the mean

DC-3's mean transit is 6.1 days versus a 5.4-day network, a 0.7-day gap that looks minor. But its standard deviation is 2.1× the network's. Roughly a third of DC-3 orders arrive within promise; the rest scatter. Cutting variance, not average speed, is the lever that moves its OTIF.

Root cause

Three reasons drive 79% of late deliveries: carrier transit overrun, late dispatch from DC, and order-hold release delay. Two of the three are internal and fixable without a carrier change. The Pareto is what turns "we have a service problem" into "fix these three things first".

Executive Brief

07 / SO WHAT

The one page that goes to the operations director. Finding, cause, recommendation, expected impact.

Findings & recommendation

The network's 82% OTIF masks a structural outlier. DC-3 carries 2.1× the lead-time variance of the network and 41% of all exceptions on 17% of volume. The cause is concentrated: single-carrier dependency on its express lane, compounded by internal dispatch delays. Re-routing that lane to the more reliable carrier and dual-sourcing the backup is projected to recover roughly six points of OTIF and lift DC-3's margin toward the network mean.

What we found
  • One DC drives 41% of exceptions on 17% of volume
  • Variance, not average speed, breaks its SLA
  • Three reason codes explain 79% of late deliveries
  • Service failure tracks the thinnest margin
What we recommend
  • Re-route DC-3 express lane to the reliable carrier
  • Dual-source the backup to remove single-point risk
  • Fix internal dispatch & order-hold release delays
  • Monitor rolling-3M OTIF per DC, not network average
